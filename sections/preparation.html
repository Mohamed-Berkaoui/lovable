Project Preparation

Great software starts long before the first line of code. Learn how to prepare your project for success.

🕑 Estimated reading time: 12 minutes

Why Preparation Matters

Imagine building a house without a blueprint. You might start with the walls, realise the door is in the wrong place, tear it down, and start over. Software works exactly the same way.

Preparation reduces waste. When you invest time upfront to clarify your goals, understand your users, and define success metrics, every minute of building time that follows becomes dramatically more productive.

This is especially true with Lovable. Because the AI builds so quickly, a vague prompt can produce a fully built — but completely wrong — application in minutes. Preparation ensures you build the right thing, not just a thing.

📚 Remember

"Weeks of coding can save you hours of planning." — an old engineering proverb that is even more relevant in the age of AI.

Understanding the Problem

Before you think about features, screens, or technology, ask yourself one question: "What problem am I solving?"

Write down the answer in one or two sentences. If you can't, you're not ready to build yet.

Problem Statement Template

Template [Target users] need a way to [action/goal] because [reason/pain point]. Currently, they [current workaround], which is [why it's inadequate].

Example

Example Freelance designers need a way to track project hours because they bill clients by the hour. Currently, they use spreadsheets, which are error-prone and lack automation.

Defining User Personas

A user persona is a fictional but realistic description of one type of person who will use your app. Personas keep your design decisions grounded in reality instead of assumptions.

What to Include in a Persona

  • Name & role: "Sarah, 32, freelance graphic designer"
  • Goals: What do they want to achieve?
  • Pain points: What frustrates them today?
  • Tech comfort: Are they tech-savvy or beginners?
  • Context: When and where will they use your app?

Example Persona

Persona Name: Sarah Role: Freelance graphic designer Age: 32 Goals: Track hours per project, generate invoices Pain Points: Spreadsheets are slow, forgets to log time Tech Comfort: Medium — uses Figma, Notion, Slack Context: Works from home, uses laptop + phone

💡 Tip

Create 2–3 personas maximum. More than that and you'll lose focus. Your MVP should serve your primary persona extremely well.

Defining Success Metrics

How will you know if your app is successful? Without metrics, you're guessing. Define measurable goals before you start building.

Types of Metrics

Metric Type Example When to Use
Acquisition 100 sign-ups in first week Validating demand
Activation 60% complete onboarding Measuring first experience
Retention 40% return within 7 days Testing stickiness
Revenue 10 paid conversions/month Business viability
Task Completion User logs time in < 30 sec UX quality

Common Mistakes

⚠️ Mistake 1: Skipping research

Building without talking to real potential users. Your assumptions about what people need are almost always wrong. Talk to at least 5 people before building anything.

⚠️ Mistake 2: Feature overload

Trying to build everything at once. Your MVP should have 3–5 core features, not 30. If you can't describe your app in one sentence, it's too complex.

⚠️ Mistake 3: Vague goals

"I want to build something cool" is not a goal. "I want freelancers to track billable hours in under 30 seconds" is a goal.

⚠️ Mistake 4: Ignoring the competition

If similar tools exist, study them. What do they do well? What do they do poorly? Your app should clearly improve on at least one dimension.

Example: Turning an Idea into a Structured Plan

Raw Idea

"I want to build a time-tracking app for freelancers."

Step-by-Step Structuring

  1. Define the problem: Freelancers lose money because they forget to log hours or use error-prone spreadsheets.
  2. Identify primary persona: Sarah, 32, freelance designer, works from home, needs mobile-friendly solution.
  3. Set success metrics: 50 sign-ups in week 1, 60% complete onboarding, average time-log action under 30 seconds.
  4. List core features (MVP): Start/stop timer, manual time entry, project categorisation, weekly summary export.
  5. List "nice-to-have" features (post-MVP): Invoice generation, client portal, integrations with Stripe/Notion.
  6. Write the problem statement: "Freelance designers need a way to track project hours because they bill clients by the hour. Currently, they use spreadsheets, which are error-prone and lack automation."

Section Summary

  1. Preparation prevents wasted build time — especially with AI tools that build fast.
  2. Start by clearly defining the problem you're solving in 1–2 sentences.
  3. Create 2–3 user personas to ground your design decisions in reality.
  4. Set measurable success metrics before writing a single prompt.
  5. Avoid common traps: feature overload, skipping research, and vague goals.
  6. Structure your raw idea into a clear plan before moving to the PRD stage.
